Before joining the competition, I talked with friends in the legal industry and identified the following problems in Hong Kong:

  • The annual PCLL quota hinders the career prospect of many legal degree graduates
  • Staff efficiency is one of the KPIs in a legal firm. The firm should have a strong demand to improve staff efficiency via digital transformation.

After 54 hours of hacking, Legot team is honored to receive the second runner up award of the first legal Hackathon in Hong Kong. Our chat-bot prototype is mainly used to improve staff efficiency and client engagement.

In retrospect, I am very impressed by this Hackathon with the following observations:

  • Good mix of participants. In this competition, 50% are developers and 30% are legal and compliance professionals. Diversity drives innovation. For example, our team has 1 UI/UX designer, 1 lawyer, 2 professional accountants, 2 developers, and several financial practitioners.
  • Useful feedback from mentors and judges. 1 technical workshop and 2 technical demonstrations were provided on Friday night. Our team received 5 rounds of face-to-face mentoring on Saturday afternoon. There was a round of pitching practice on Sunday morning, which helped us prepare and refine the pitch. In addition, judges kindly pointed out the pros/cons of our prototype after the judging session. Their feedback was useful for us to further develop the prototype after the competition.
  • Venue and Food. Thomson Reuters offered participants access to their office space at Central until 9pm everyday during Hackathon. Each team had a dedicated room for brainstorming, discussion, and prototyping. In addition, participants had 7 full meals plus an unlimited supply of snack and drink. Teams can be focused on developing innovative ideas with minimal distraction.
  • Excellent WIFI. There were 7 WIFI hotspots provided by Thomson Reuters. WIFI connection was never an issue during the competition.

No Hackathon is perfect. I observed a few minor issues:

  • Topics are too general. It was especially difficult for our large team (with 9 people from diversified background) to narrow down the scope. For example, we spent 1 addition hour after the venue close at 9pm on Friday night, and debated on Saturday morning in order to finalize the direction.
  • Lack of legal support staff as participant. In my opinion, a paralegal/ legal clerk may have a different view than a practicing lawyer.

A 54-hour legal hack won’t make any significant change in the whole legal industry in Hong Kong. Yet, it is a starting point for the passionate legal experts and leaders to work with tech talent and other stakeholders to explore ways to solve problems in Hong Kong legal industry. In addition to this competition, there are several related activities in the coming months (e.g. a legal Hackathon organised by Hong Kong Law Society at Cyberport).

As a Cyberport startup co-founder and an executive committee member of Cyberport Startup Alumni Association (CSAA), I know a few LegalTech startups before (e.g. Zegal, previously named as Dragon Law). With these competitions as catalyst, I foresee that several innovative LegalTech ideas may be evolved as startups or sustainable businesses. LegalTech should have a chance to bring new career opportunities for legal /tech talent in Hong Kong.
